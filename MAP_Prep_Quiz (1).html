Passage 1 — Literary · "The Tell-Tale Heart" by Edgar Allan Poe
The Tell-Tale Heart (excerpt)

1TRUE! — nervous — very, very dreadfully nervous I had been and am; but why WILL you say that I am mad? The disease had sharpened my senses — not destroyed — not dulled them. Above all was the sense of hearing acute. I heard all things in the heaven and in the earth. I heard many things in hell. How, then, am I mad? Hearken! and observe how healthily — how calmly I can tell you the whole story.

2It is impossible to say how first the idea entered my brain; but once conceived, it haunted me day and night. Object there was none. Passion there was none. I loved the old man. He had never wronged me. He had never given me insult. For his gold I had no desire. I think it was his eye! yes, it was this! He had the eye of a vulture — a pale blue eye, with a film over it. 3You fancy me mad. Madmen know nothing. But you should have seen me. You should have seen how wisely I proceeded — with what caution — with what foresight — with what dissimulation I went to work!

Passage 2 — Informational · "The Psychology of Suspense"
Before answering: Read the full paired text on CommonLit (paired with "The Tell-Tale Heart"). Key paragraphs are below for reference.

1Why do people willingly seek out frightening stories, horror films, and suspenseful novels — even though these experiences cause real stress? The answer lies in the brain's ancient threat-detection system, which evolved long before humans invented storytelling.

2When a person encounters uncertainty — not knowing what will happen next — the brain triggers the same physiological response it uses to detect predators: elevated heart rate, heightened attention, and the release of adrenaline.

3Suspense in fiction exploits this system deliberately. A skilled author introduces a threat, delays its resolution, and withholds just enough information to keep the reader in a state of productive uncertainty.

4This gap between knowing something is fake and feeling it is real is called "fictional immersion," and research suggests it is what makes certain stories linger in the mind long after they are finished.

5Studies show that readers who regularly engage with suspenseful fiction demonstrate higher levels of empathy and cognitive flexibility than those who do not.

Passage 1 — Literary · "Lather and Nothing Else" by Hernando Téllez

1He came in without a word. I was stropping my best razor. And when I recognized him, I started to tremble. But he did not notice. To cover my nervousness, I went on honing the razor. Captain Torres was in the habit of coming to my shop every two weeks for a shave, and I was an enemy of his — or rather, he was my enemy.

2I began to work carefully on his beard. My hands started trembling again. The man could not have noticed it — that was fortunate for me. I wished he had not come in. Probably many of the men he had ordered shot had been afraid, just as I was now.

3What do you want to do with him? Kill him? No. And just what would you do with the body? Where would you hide it? You would have to flee. Leave all this behind. No. You don't have the guts for that. It would be better to let things take their course. Don't put yourself in a position of having to kill again.

4I am a revolutionary, not a murderer. And it would be so easy to kill him. I am a good barber. The best in this town, if I may say so myself. He won't feel a thing. He'll die well. But I don't want to be a barber who shaves the corpse of his victim.

5"You'll be given a medal, friend," Torres said when the shave was over. "You didn't kill me. Everyone said you would. But it's not easy to kill. I know what I'm talking about."
